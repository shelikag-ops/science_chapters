===== SLIDE-3 =====
💡 Key Idea
What IS a Category?
A category = a group of things that share something in common
🍎🍌🍇
Fruits
Apple, banana, grapes…
They all grow on plants and taste sweet
🐕🐈🐇
Pets
Dog, cat, rabbit…
They all live in our homes with us
✏️📏📐
School Supplies
Pencil, ruler, eraser…
They all help you learn and create
🚗🚌🚲
Transport
Car, bus, bicycle…
They all help you get somewhere
🔑 Big Idea: A category needs 
at least 2 things
 that share 
a similarity or purpose
.
And here's the cool part — the same item can belong to different categories depending on HOW you're looking at it!
🎲

===== SLIDE-8 =====
🔍 Real Life Mysteries
Can You Crack These?
Think carefully before revealing the answer — these need REAL thinking!
Mystery 01
🏪 A new helper starts at the grocery store. They put the toothpaste next to the cheese, shampoo next to the apples, and soap near the bread. Customers are furious. Why is this so bad, and what went wrong in the helper's thinking?
🤔 I've thought about it — reveal!
The helper didn't think about 
purpose categories
. Customers expect things grouped by what they ARE (food vs. hygiene). When categories are wrong, nobody can find anything! Organisation only works when the system makes sense to the people using it.
Mystery 02
📚 A librarian organised ALL the books by colour — red books together, blue books together, yellow books together. The library looks beautiful! But students are complaining they can't find anything. Why is a beautiful system sometimes a BAD system?
🤔 I've thought about it — reveal!
Organisation must match its 
purpose
! The purpose of a library is to find books by topic or author — not by colour. A pretty system that doesn't help people do what they came to do is actually a useless system.
Mystery 03
🧩 Scientists studying animals have a category called "mammals." A whale lives in the ocean and a bat flies in the sky. Yet BOTH are called mammals — not fish, not birds. What must mammals share that makes them belong to the same category despite looking so different?
🤔 I've thought about it — reveal!
Categories are based on 
shared hidden rules
, not just looks! Mammals all: have fur/hair, are warm-blooded, give birth to live babies, and feed their babies milk. Whales and bats both do ALL of these things — even though one swims and one flies.
Mystery 04
🧺 Your family is sorting laundry: whites, darks, colours. Where does a light grey shirt with a dark blue stripe go? There's no perfect category for it! What does this teach us about real-life categories?
🤔 I've thought about it — reveal!
Real life has 
"oddball" items
 that don't fit perfectly into any category. This is normal! When you find an oddball, you have choices: (1) create a new category, (2) put it in the closest group, or (3) decide which rule matters most. Scientists face this too — and it often leads to new discoveries!
Mystery 05
🧠 Mia tries to memorise a shopping list of 18 random items and forgets most of them. Then she groups them into "vegetables," "dairy," and "snacks." Now she remembers almost all of them. She didn't learn any new facts — so what actually changed?
🤔 I've thought about it — reveal!
She changed how the information was 
organised in her brain
! Your brain can hold about 4–7 "chunks" of information at once. 18 random items = 18 chunks (too many). 3 groups = 3 chunks (easy!). Organisation doesn't add information — it makes existing information easier to hold and find.
Mystery 06
🔬 A scientist discovers a new creature in the Amazon rainforest. It has scales like a fish, lays eggs like a reptile, can breathe underwater like a fish, but walks on land like a lizard. The scientist needs to categorise it. What problem does she face, and how might she solve it?
🤔 I've thought about it — reveal!
She faces a genuine scientific puzzle — the creature shares features of 
multiple existing categories
. She could: (1) choose the category with the MOST features in common, (2) decide which feature is most fundamental, or (3) create a brand new category! This actually happened when scientists first saw a platypus — they thought someone was playing a trick on them!

===== SLIDE-10 =====
🏠 Homework
Your Category Detective Missions!
5 real-life investigations to do at home this week
1
🍽️
The Dinner Table Sort
At dinner tonight, look at everything on the table. Secretly sort ALL items into at least 3 different categories (you decide the rules!). Tell someone in your family — can they guess your sorting rule? Try to find at least 2 different ways to sort the same items.
2
🚿
Bathroom Cabinet Detective
Look inside your bathroom cabinet or under the sink. Draw or write down everything you find. Then ask: is it organised? What categories would YOU use to reorganise it better? Present your plan to a parent — would it actually work?
3
🌳
Nature's Categories Outside
On your next walk outside (or just look out the window), find 10 things in nature. Sort them into categories — but you have to find at least 3 DIFFERENT ways to sort the SAME 10 things. What do different sorting systems reveal about nature?
4
🎲
Family Memory Tray Game
Play the Memory Tray game with your family! Put 15–20 objects on a tray. Give everyone 30 seconds to look, then hide the tray. Who remembers the most? Teach your family the "secret" — group items into categories first! Did it help them?
5
📓
My Observation Diary: Oddball Hunt
Over 3 days, keep a small diary. Each day, find ONE "oddball" — something in real life that doesn't fit neatly into any obvious category (like a tomato: is it a fruit or vegetable?). Write down: what it is, why it's hard to categorise, and what YOU would decide. Bring your 3 oddballs to the next class!
