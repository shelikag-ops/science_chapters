📘 Session 1 of 2What Is Gravity?

🔭 Today's puzzle

What if gravity STOPPED for 10 seconds?

Right now you're sitting (or standing). Your drink is in its glass. The dog is on the floor. The Earth is going around the Sun. All normal.

Now imagine gravity just turned off. For 10 seconds. What happens?

🤔 Today's mission: meet gravity — the invisible force that holds your feet to the floor, the Moon to Earth, and Earth to the Sun.
🍎⬇️

📜 So what IS gravity?

The big idea:
Gravity is a FORCE — an invisible pull. Every object with mass pulls every other object with mass.

➡ Earth has LOTS of mass. So it pulls everything toward its center — that's why things fall "down."
➡ You have a tiny bit of mass too. So you pull on Earth! (But way too small to notice.)
➡ The bigger the mass, the bigger the pull.

Gravity is what gives your feet weight, what holds the air around our planet, and what keeps the Moon orbiting Earth.

🍎 MYSTERY #1: Newton's Apple

🕵️ Real-life puzzle

The famous story: Isaac Newton (yes, the same Newton from C-5/C-6/C-7!) was sitting under a tree. An apple fell on his head. He asked a strange question:

"If gravity pulls the apple down to Earth — does it also pull the MOON?"

If yes — why doesn't the moon FALL on us, like the apple?

The Moon IS falling toward Earth — all the time! But it's also moving sideways really, really fast. So as it falls, it keeps missing Earth. It curves around us instead. That's called an orbit. Same with Earth around the Sun, satellites around Earth, even tiny dust grains around big planets. Falling + sideways speed = orbiting!

🧪 Demo: Drop Test

Materials: a small rock (or coin) · a tightly crumpled ball of paper

  1. Hold both at the SAME height. (One in each hand.)
  2. Drop them at exactly the same time.
  3. Listen — do they hit the ground at the same time? Or does the heavier one land first?
💭 Predict first: Heavy = faster? Or do they tie?
Then check: They land at the same time. Wild, right? Gravity pulls on EVERYTHING the same way. (Air can mess this up — that's why we used a crumpled paper ball, not a flat sheet.)

🍃 MYSTERY #2: Why does paper float and a coin DROP fast?

🕵️ Real-life puzzle

Drop a flat sheet of paper. It floats and flutters down slowly.

Drop a coin. It plummets.

But you JUST learned that gravity pulls everything the same. So what gives??

It's not gravity that makes the difference — it's AIR! Flat paper has lots of surface, so air resistance pushes back hard, slowing it. The coin has tiny surface area — air can't do much. In a vacuum (no air), they would fall at the EXACT same speed. Astronauts proved this on the Moon — feather + hammer = same landing time!

🌬 The Air Trick

Gravity always pulls equally. But on Earth, AIR gets in the way — sometimes a lot, sometimes a little.

🪶 Feather
Lots of surface, very light. Air pushes back hard → falls slowly.
🪨 Rock
Compact, heavy. Air barely matters → falls fast.
📄 Paper (flat)
Maximum air resistance → drifts down.
📄 Paper (crumpled)
Less surface → falls fast like a rock.
💡 If we removed the air, ALL of these would fall at the same rate.

🌍 MYSTERY #3: Where does gravity COME from?

🕵️ Real-life puzzle

You can't see gravity. You can't hear it. Yet it pulls everything down. Where is it hiding? What MAKES it?

MASS makes gravity. Anything with mass has gravity — even YOU! The more mass, the stronger the pull. Earth is HUGE (5,972,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kg), so its pull is strong enough to hold us down. But your body has gravity too — it's just unbelievably tiny because you have very little mass compared to Earth.

Even better: scientists think gravity actually bends space itself — but that's for D-7 in a few weeks!

🎉 End of Session 1

You've met gravity — and learned the air trick.

✓ Gravity = invisible force pulling things with mass together
✓ Earth's gravity pulls everything toward its center ("down")
✓ All objects fall at the SAME rate — air just messes it up sometimes

📘 Session 2 of 2Gravity Across the Universe

🌑 The Moon has gravity too — just less.

The Moon is much smaller than Earth (about 1/6 the mass). So its gravity is much weaker.

👤 Your weight on Earth
If you weigh 30 kg here…
🌑 Your weight on the Moon
You'd weigh about 5 kg there. Six times lighter!
🪐 On Jupiter (huge!)
You'd weigh 70 kg. Two-and-a-half times heavier!
🌞 On the Sun (insane mass)
You'd weigh ~840 kg. (You'd also be vaporized — but still!)

🌑 MYSTERY #4: Why do you weigh LESS on the Moon?

🕵️ Real-life puzzle

Astronauts on the Moon look almost like they're in slow motion. They take giant fluffy hops. Why?

Less gravity! The Moon has only 1/6 of Earth's gravity (because it's much smaller). So when an astronaut jumps with the same leg push, they go 6× higher and float down 6× slower. Same astronaut. Same body. Different planet — different gravity.

Important note: their mass didn't change (they have the same body). Their weight changed. Mass = how much stuff. Weight = how hard gravity pulls on that stuff.

🚀 MYSTERY #5: Why do astronauts FLOAT in space?

🕵️ Real-life puzzle

Astronauts on the International Space Station float around inside. Many people think it's because there's no gravity in space. That's wrong! The ISS is only 400 km up — well within Earth's strong gravity.

So if there IS gravity… why aren't they pulled down?

They're ALL falling — the astronauts AND the station — at the same rate. Just like the Moon: they're falling toward Earth, but moving sideways so fast they keep missing. Inside the falling station, they appear to float relative to the floor.

It's the same feeling as the top of a roller coaster drop — for a split second, you feel weightless. Astronauts feel that all the time!

🎢 Demo: Make a Free-Fall Bottle

Materials: empty plastic water bottle · water · push pin or sharp pencil

  1. Outside (or over the sink!), poke 2 small holes near the bottom of the bottle.
  2. Cover the holes with your fingers. Fill the bottle.
  3. Hold it up high. Let go of your fingers — water sprays out. Why? Gravity pulls water down through the holes.
  4. Now: holding the bottle, DROP it (or toss it gently up). While it falls — look at the holes!
  5. Surprise: the water STOPS coming out!
💡 Why? While the bottle falls, the water and bottle are falling together — same as the astronauts in the ISS! There's no "down" relative to the bottle, so the water doesn't leak. Tiny experiment, BIG idea.

🌞 MYSTERY #6: Why doesn't Earth fall onto the Sun?

🕵️ Real-life puzzle

The Sun has 333,000 times the mass of Earth. So its gravity is HUGELY stronger. It's definitely pulling Earth toward it. So why don't we crash into the Sun?

Same reason as the Moon and Earth! Earth IS falling toward the Sun — but moving sideways (around the Sun) at about 107,000 km per hour. That sideways speed keeps Earth in orbit. Without it, we'd spiral into the Sun in about 2 months. With it, we keep curving around — once a year. That's your year!

🌌 Gravity is everywhere

⚖️ Mass vs Weight (a sneak preview)

In this chapter we've mixed two words. They're actually different — and it's a big deal.

MASS
How much "stuff" is in your body. NEVER changes — same on Earth, Moon, anywhere.
WEIGHT
How hard gravity pulls on that stuff. CHANGES depending on the gravity around you.

✨ What you now know

  • Gravity = invisible force pulling things with mass toward each other
  • Earth's gravity pulls everything toward its center (= "down")
  • All objects fall at the same rate — air resistance is what makes light/flat things fall slowly
  • More mass = stronger gravity
  • Astronauts in orbit don't have "no gravity" — they're in free fall around Earth
  • Earth orbits the Sun the same way the Moon orbits Earth
  • Mass ≠ Weight (we'll learn more in D-7!)
