CHAPTER 12 · ENERGY & FORCE
💪 ⚡ 🤜🤛

Force vs. Energy

Two science words people mix up all the time. Today we crack them.

🎬 1 video · 🕵️ 3 mysteries · 🎲 Push-pull demo

🔭 Today's question

You push a wall as hard as you can. The wall doesn't move. Did you use ENERGY?

Tricky! You're definitely tired. You felt strain. Sweat is dripping. But the wall didn't budge an inch.

🤔 Today's mission: tell apart FORCE (the push) and ENERGY (the work that gets done). Hint: they're not the same.
🧒💨🧱

💪 What's a FORCE?

A force is a push or pull. That's it. Anywhere there's a push or pull happening, there's a force.

🛒 Shopping cart — you PUSH it (force)
🪢 Tug of war — both teams PULL (force)
🌬 Wind — air molecules PUSH things (force)
🌎 Gravity — Earth PULLS you down (force)
🧲 Magnet — PULLS metals (force)
📏 Stretched spring — PULLS back to its shape (force)
📝 Force is measured in Newtons (named after Isaac Newton). 1 Newton ≈ the force of holding a small apple. Your weight is your gravity-force in Newtons.

⚡ How is Energy DIFFERENT?

Force is the PUSH itself — how hard you press.
Energy is whether anything actually MOVES because of that push.

The key difference:
💪 Force = how hard you push or pull (just the squeeze)
⚡ Energy = movement happens because of the force (something goes somewhere)

Wall pushing

Big force ✅
Wall didn't move ❌
So almost no useful energy was transferred to the wall.

Pushing a cart

Same force ✅
Cart rolls ✅
Lots of energy went into making it move!

⚙️ Force = the Pusher · Energy = the Power

Most kids (and grown-ups!) use FORCE and ENERGY as if they mean the same thing. They DON'T. This is the #1 SciOly trap.

👷 Mental model: Imagine a worker on a construction site.
• FORCE = the worker's shove — measured in Newtons (N). How HARD does the push feel?
• ENERGY = the worker's fuel/breakfast — measured in Joules (J). How much WORK can be done?
• WORK = what actually got DONE = Force × Distance moved.
Question scientists askQuantityUnit
"How HARD did you push?"ForceNewtons (N)
"How much WORK got done?"Work / EnergyJoules (J)
"How FAST did the push become work?"PowerWatts (W)
🚨 Misconception alert: "I pushed REALLY hard, so I used a LOT of energy." ❌
Truth: you used a lot of force. Whether you used a lot of ENERGY depends on whether anything MOVED. Pushing a wall = lots of force, ZERO work, zero energy delivered to the wall.
🎲 ACTIVITY · 5 MIN

Push the Wall vs. Push the Box

Try this:

  1. Push a wall hard for 30 seconds. Stop. Did the wall move? Are you tired?
  2. Now push a small box across the floor. Did the box move? Are you tired?

You used FORCE in both cases. But energy was only transferred to the BOX (which moved). The wall just gave you a hot face!

👩‍🏫 Teacher note
Teacher: The body's "tiredness" comes from internal energy use — your muscles burn calories to apply the force. But that energy goes to HEAT inside you (you get warm), not into the wall. Scientists are strict: useful energy = something moves. Heat is "wasted" energy in this context.

🧲 Forces You CAN'T Touch · Spiral to A-5A

Most forces happen by CONTACT — your hand touches the box, the wind touches the leaf. But some forces work without touching at all.

🤚 CONTACT forces

You shove a cart. Wind pushes a sail. A wall pushes back on you. Friction grips a tire. Need physical touch.

👻 NON-CONTACT forces

Magnetism (🔁 A-5A!) — magnet pulls a nail across a table without touching it. Gravity — Earth pulls the Moon from 384,000 km away. Static electricity — balloon pulls hair without touching.

🔁 Spiral to A-5A Magnets: remember the magnetic FIELD around a magnet? That field IS the force, projected through empty space. Even though nothing visible is "touching", energy is being transferred through the field. This is the same idea behind gravity, electricity, and even radio waves.
🎯 SciOly trick question: A magnet on a table pulls a paperclip 5 cm closer. Was a force exerted? Was work done?
Show answerYES to both! Force = magnetic pull (no contact needed). Work = force × distance moved = real Joules of energy delivered to the paperclip's motion.
🕵️ MYSTERY #1 · THINK BEFORE YOU CLICK

The Tired Holder

You hold a heavy book in your outstretched hand for 5 minutes. Your arm is BURNING. But the book never moved. According to physics, did you do any "work"?

🎯 Surprising answer: NO! Physics says "work" only counts if something moves. You applied force (pushing the book up against gravity) — but the book didn't move. So zero "work" in physics terms. Why are you tired? Your muscles wasted lots of energy as HEAT just to maintain the push. The energy went into your body, not into moving the book. Physicists are strict: no movement = no work.

⚖️ Balanced Forces (Why things DON'T move)

When you push a wall, the wall pushes BACK with the SAME force. Two forces cancel out. Nothing moves.

📜 Newton's Big Rule: for every push there's an equal push BACK. Walls. Floors. The ground when you stand on it. Every time.
🪑 Sitting on a chair
Why don't you fall through?
Gravity pushes you DOWN. Chair pushes UP equally hard. Cancels out. You don't move (vertical-wise).
🪢 Tug of war (tied)
Both teams pulling, no one moves
Both teams pull with equal force. Forces cancel. Until one team's pull beats the other.

🧮 Scientific Work — the Hidden Formula

When physicists say "WORK", they mean something very specific. Here's the secret math.

⚙️ WORK = FORCE × DISTANCE moved.
(5 Newtons of push, moving the box 2 meters → 5 × 2 = 10 Joules of work done.)
ActionForce (N)Distance (m)Work (J)
Push toy car gently2 N3 m6 J
Push the SAME car twice as hard4 N3 m12 J ← doubled
Push gently but for 2× the distance2 N6 m12 J ← also doubled
Push a wall HARD for an hour100 N0 m0 J ← ZERO!
🔑 Pattern: double the FORCE → double the work. Double the DISTANCE → double the work. Zero distance → ZERO work, no matter how hard the push.
This is why "physics work" ≠ "feeling tired". Tired arms = wasted heat inside YOU, not work done on the world.
🎯 SciOly question: Sarah pushes a box with 8 N of force across a 5-meter floor. How many Joules of work did she do?
Show answer8 × 5 = 40 J. (If the box only moved 1 m before getting stuck, she only did 8 J of work — the rest of her effort went to heat from friction!)

🚀 Unbalanced Forces (Movement!)

When forces DON'T cancel out — when one is bigger — THINGS MOVE.

🚗 Car accelerating — engine push > friction pull → moves forward
🍎 Falling apple — gravity pull > air resistance → falls
🎈 Balloon rising — air buoyancy > gravity → goes up
🪁 Kite flying — wind push > gravity + string pull → stays up
💡 The Big Idea: if forces are balanced, an object stays still (or keeps moving the same way). If forces are unbalanced, the object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.
🕵️ MYSTERY #2 · THINK BEFORE YOU CLICK

The Floor That Pushes You Back

You're standing on the ground. Earth's gravity is pulling you down hard — about 700 Newtons of force on an adult. But you're not flying down to Earth's center. Why not?

🎯 The floor (or ground) is pushing UP on you with EXACTLY the same 700 Newtons! It's a balanced force. The ground feels solid because the molecules inside resist being squished. Step off a cliff and there's no upward push anymore — gravity wins, and you fall. You can FEEL this when you jump on a trampoline: the trampoline's upward push briefly OUTWEIGHS gravity, throwing you up.
🕵️ MYSTERY #3 · THINK BEFORE YOU CLICK

The Slick Floor Surprise

You push a heavy box on a normal floor — slow, hard work. Same box on a slippery icy floor — easy! Same force, very different result. Why?

🎯 The floor was pushing BACK on the box (a force called FRICTION). On a normal floor, friction is large — your push has to overcome it before the box moves. On ice, friction is tiny — almost all your force goes into MOVING the box. Same push, different opposing force. This is why slippery is "easy" but also dangerous: less friction = less control.

🚛 The Heavy Wagon Challenge — Variable Swap

Same FORCE. Different MASS. What happens?

🔬 Imagine this experiment: You walk up to a TOY car (mass = 1 kg) and push it with 10 N of force. Then you walk up to a REAL car (mass = 1,000 kg) and push it with the SAME 10 N of force.
ObjectMassSame ForceWhat happens?
🪀 Toy car1 kg10 NZOOMS! Big speed-up.
🚙 Real car1,000 kg10 NBarely moves. Tiny speed-up.
🔑 The Big Discovery: MASS = resistance to force. The bigger the mass, the more force you need to get the same speed-up.

Newton's Second Law (a sneak peek!): Force = Mass × Acceleration
Same Force → Bigger Mass → Smaller Acceleration. That's why 1 kid can push a shopping cart, but 10 kids needed to push a stalled car.
🎯 SciOly extrapolation: If you can give a 1 kg ball an acceleration of 5 m/s² with your push, what acceleration would the SAME push give a 5 kg ball?
Show answer1 m/s² — five times the mass, one-fifth the acceleration. Mass divides the effect of force.

✨ What you now know

  • 💪 Force = a push or pull
  • ⚡ Energy = the actual movement that happens (or work done)
  • 📏 Force is measured in Newtons
  • ⚖️ Balanced forces → nothing changes
  • 🚀 Unbalanced forces → speed up / slow down / change direction
  • 🪑 The floor really IS pushing you up
  • 🧲 Pushing a wall hard = lots of force, no movement, no work
