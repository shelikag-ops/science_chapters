Vibrations & Pitch

Pluck a string. It blurs. It hums. Why??

🎬 1 video · 🎲 Rubber band orchestra · 🕵️ 2 mysteries

🔭 Today's puzzle

Touch a guitar string. Now pluck it.

When still: silent. When plucked: a NOTE comes out. Where did the note come from? The wood didn't change. The string didn't appear.

🤔 Today's mission: figure out the secret of how sound is made — and why your dog can hear noises you can't.
🎸

The Big Secret: VIBRATIONS 🌀

When you pluck the string, it goes back and forth REALLY fast — too fast to see clearly. It looks blurry.

That super-fast back-and-forth motion is called VIBRATION. And vibrations make sound.

🔑 Rule #1: Sound comes from vibrations. Always. No vibration → no sound.

Touch a vibrating string with your finger. The blur stops. The sound stops. That's how you know!

🎲 ACTIVITY · 15 MIN · HANDS-ON

The Rubber Band Orchestra

Grab a few rubber bands of different sizes. Stretch one between your fingers, hold close to your ear, and pluck.

  • 🎵 Hear the sound? Now LOOK at the band — see the blur?
  • 📏 Try a thicker band: higher or lower note?
  • 🤏 Try stretching tighter: higher or lower note?
  • 👆 Touch the band gently — sound stops! Why?
👩‍🏫 Teacher note
Teacher: Tighter or thinner band = higher pitch (faster vibration). Thicker or looser = lower pitch (slower vibration). Touching = damping = stops vibration = no sound. This is the foundation for understanding all stringed instruments.

🚫 No Air, No Noise — the Concept

Here's the BIG IDEA most people get wrong. Sound is NOT a thing that travels by itself like a ball thrown through space. Sound is a relay race.

🏃 Sound = a chain of particle bumps.
A vibrating object pushes the air molecule next to it. That molecule bumps the next. That one bumps the next. The wiggle gets passed down the line — like dominoes — until it reaches your eardrum.
🔔 → 🟢→🟢→🟢→🟢 → 👂
🤔 Common WRONG idea: "Sound is a thing that flies through the air, like an invisible bullet." ❌
Right idea: "Sound is a wiggle being PASSED through stuff. The stuff (air, water, wood) doesn't travel — only the wiggle does." ✅
🚀 Big consequence: If there are NO particles to bump (like in outer space), there can be NO sound. We'll see this in Session 2 when we ask: can you hear an explosion in space?

PITCH = how FAST something vibrates 📏

Some sounds are HIGH (squeaky mouse 🐭). Some are LOW (deep growl 🦁). The difference?

HIGH PITCH
Fast vibrations = many wiggles per second
LOW PITCH
Slow vibrations = few wiggles per second
📏 The science word: we call "wiggles per second" the frequency. High frequency = high pitch. Measured in Hertz (Hz).

LOUDNESS = how BIG the vibration is 📢

Same string, gentle pluck = quiet. Hard pluck = loud. Same pitch — different loudness.

QUIET sound
Small wiggles (small amplitude)
LOUD sound
Big wiggles (big amplitude)
🔑 Two dials on every sound: the frequency dial (pitch) and the amplitude dial (loudness). They're independent.
🕵️ MYSTERY #1 · THINK FIRST

Why are big bells lower than small bells?

Church bells are HUGE and sound DEEP. Tiny bicycle bells are small and sound HIGH. Why does size affect pitch?

🎯 Bigger objects vibrate more slowly than smaller ones. Slow vibration = low frequency = low pitch. Same with strings: thick guitar strings (low E) vibrate slowly = low note. Thin strings (high E) vibrate fast = high note. Same with people: bigger vocal cords (deeper voice) vs smaller (higher voice). Size + tension = pitch.

🎵 How instruments work

Every musical instrument is just a way to make controlled vibrations:

🎸 Guitar
String vibrates → wood box amplifies the sound (makes it louder).
🥁 Drum
Skin vibrates when hit. Big drum = low note. Small drum = high note.
🎺 Trumpet
Lips vibrate, air column inside the tube vibrates with them.
🎤 Voice
Vocal cords vibrate as you push air through. Tighten = higher.
🔔 Bell
Whole metal shape vibrates after being struck.
🕵️ MYSTERY #2 · THINK FIRST

The Singing Wine Glass

A wet finger rubbed around the rim of a wine glass makes a clear musical note. There's no string, no drum skin, no reed. What's vibrating?

🎯 The glass itself is vibrating! Your finger drags slightly, then slips, drags, slips — these tiny stick-slip events cause the whole glass to ring. Add water and the pitch CHANGES (more water = more mass = slower vibration = lower note). Try it with several glasses at different water levels — you've made an instrument!

⚡ The Lightning Count — Sound's Secret Speed

Sound is fast — but NOTHING compared to light. Here's how to use that gap to MEASURE distance.

📏 Speed of sound in air ≈ 343 meters per second (about 340 m/s).
⚡ Speed of light: 300,000,000 m/s (basically instant for anything you can see).

The Lightning Trick: when you see a flash, count seconds until you hear the boom. Each second ≈ 340 meters away. That's the distance to the lightning!

Seconds countedDistance (m)Roughly
1 sec340 m3 football fields
3 sec~1,020 m≈ 1 km
5 sec~1,700 m≈ 1.7 km
10 sec~3,400 m≈ 3.4 km
🧮 The math: distance = speed × time. So if time = 4 sec, distance = 340 × 4 = 1,360 m. (🔁 Spiral to D-5 Time — measuring time + speed gives us distance!)
🎯 SciOly question: You see lightning, and 6 seconds later you hear thunder. How far away is the storm?
Show answer340 × 6 = 2,040 meters (≈ 2 km). If the gap shrinks each time you count, the storm is getting CLOSER.

How Sound Travels

From a vibration over THERE… to your eardrum HERE… in 1/100th of a second.

🕵️ 4 mysteries · Activity: telephone

Sound rides through AIR 🌬

Air is full of tiny molecules (we learned this in A-3 and A-4!). When something vibrates:

  1. It pushes nearby air molecules away
  2. Those molecules bump the next ones
  3. Which bump the next ones
  4. … and so on, in a wave
  5. Eventually some hit your eardrum
🔑 Sound is a CHAIN of bumps through air molecules. It's a WAVE — like dominoes falling, or a ripple in water.
🎲 ACTIVITY · 10 MIN

The String Telephone

Take 2 paper cups + a long string (~3m).

  1. Poke a hole in the bottom of each cup.
  2. Thread the string through both, knot the ends.
  3. Each person holds a cup, walk apart until string is TIGHT.
  4. One person whispers into their cup. The other listens through theirs.
  5. You can hear them clearly! Why??
👩‍🏫 Teacher note
Teacher: The voice vibrates the cup bottom → vibrates the string → vibrates the other cup → into the listener's ear. Sound travels FASTER through solids (the string) than through air. Loose string = no transfer (vibrations die). Tight = perfect transfer.

Sound also travels through… 🌊

🌊 Water
YES. Whales sing songs across oceans. Sound travels FASTER and FARTHER in water than in air.
🪵 Wood
YES. Press your ear to a wooden table — anyone tapping it on the other end? You'll hear it loud.
🧱 Walls
YES. Why you hear neighbors' music through the wall.
🚀 Empty space (vacuum)
NO!!! No molecules = no chain of bumps = no sound. Space is totally silent.
📝 Big rule: sound needs SOMETHING to travel through. Air, water, glass, wood — anything with molecules. With nothing? Total silence.
🕵️ MYSTERY #3 · THINK FIRST

Movie Lies: Space Battles

In Star Wars, you hear EXPLOSIONS in space — laser blasts, ships crashing. But scientists say space is silent. What's going on?

🎯 Movies are lying for fun. In real space, between planets, there's almost NO air — just empty vacuum. Sound waves can't travel without molecules to bump. So a real space battle would be eerily silent. But silence is BORING in movies, so directors add sound effects. Astronauts on the ISS can hear each other ONLY because the inside of the station has air. The outside? Pure silence.

👂 Inside your amazing ear

When sound waves reach you:

  1. The outer ear (the floppy bit) funnels sound in.
  2. It hits your eardrum — a thin skin that vibrates like a tiny drum.
  3. 3 tiny bones inside (smallest in the body!) pick up and amplify the vibration.
  4. It reaches the cochlea (a curl filled with liquid).
  5. Tiny hairs sense the vibration and send a SIGNAL to your brain.
  6. Your brain says: "That's a dog barking" or "Mom is calling me."
🤯 Wild fact: the 3 tiniest bones in your body — hammer, anvil, stirrup — fit on a fingertip. They've been shaping every sound you've ever heard.
🕵️ MYSTERY #4 · THINK FIRST

The Dog Whistle

You blow a "silent" dog whistle and your dog goes nuts. But you hear nothing. The whistle is making sound — but you can't hear it. Why??

🎯 Different animals can hear different ranges of pitches. Humans hear from about 20 Hz (very low) to 20,000 Hz (very high). Dogs hear up to 45,000 Hz. The whistle makes a sound around 25,000–35,000 Hz — way above your top limit, but well within the dog's range. Bats can hear up to 200,000 Hz! Mice scream loudly to each other in pitches we can't hear at all. The world is FULL of sounds we just can't access.
🕵️ MYSTERY #5 · THINK FIRST

The Echo

You yell in a big empty cave: "HELLO!" A second later: "...hello..." comes back to you. Where did the second hello come from?

🎯 Sound BOUNCES off walls. Your "HELLO" travels out, hits a hard cave wall, and bounces back to you. The delay is because sound takes time to travel — about 1 second to go and come back from a wall ~170m away. That's an ECHO. Bats and dolphins use echoes to "see" — they make clicks, listen for the bounce, and figure out where things are. It's called echolocation.
🕵️ MYSTERY #6 · THINK FIRST

Why is sound louder at night?

Have you noticed sounds carry FARTHER and seem louder at night? Even though the sounds themselves haven't changed?

🎯 Two reasons. (1) Cooler night air bends sound waves back DOWN toward the ground (in the day, hot rising air bends them up). So at night, more sound reaches your ears. (2) The world is much QUIETER at night — fewer cars, fewer people. So one sound stands out way more without competing noise. Same physics + less background noise = sounds seem to travel forever.

✨ What you now know

  • 🌀 Sound = vibrations
  • 📏 Pitch depends on speed of vibration (frequency)
  • 📢 Loudness depends on size of vibration (amplitude)
  • 🌬 Sound travels through air, water, and solids — anything with molecules
  • 🚀 Sound CANNOT travel through empty space (vacuum)
  • 👂 Your ear: outer → eardrum → 3 tiny bones → cochlea → brain
  • 🐶 Different animals hear different pitch ranges (dogs & bats hear higher than humans)
  • ↩ Sound bounces off walls = echo
